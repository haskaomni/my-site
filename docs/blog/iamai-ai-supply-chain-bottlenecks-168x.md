## From AlphaGo to All-in NVIDIA: Iamai's Investment Awakening

**Mr. Z:** First, please introduce yourself. How did you enter the AI field? Your bio mentions Post-AGI, complex systems, and futurist. What do those labels mean?

**Iamai:** Hello everyone. I mainly do research on the AI supply chain. The earliest point was after ChatGPT came out in 2023, when I invested in NVIDIA relatively early. Since then, I have been paying attention to the entire AI supply chain.

The biggest inspiration for me probably began in 2016, when AlphaGo played Lee Sedol. From that point on, I suddenly became alert: AI would definitely, at some point in the future, break everyone's understanding by an exponential amount. Then, at the end of 2022, when ChatGPT came out, I tried using it and was extremely shocked. During that period, I was very feverishly reading AI-related things.

At that time, I suddenly realized a problem: when such a huge trend appears, there must be some kind of structural investment opportunity. I noticed NVIDIA, started buying it, and basically went all-in with my entire U.S. stock account. From then until now, it has continued to rise.

I write Post-AGI in my bio because I think AGI is something that will inevitably happen. All of my thinking and decisions are made by looking back at current investment decisions from a Post-AGI era, meaning an era after AGI.

As for complex systems, it was originally an academic discipline with an entire framework, including a book called *Complexity* and another called *Godel, Escher, Bach*. Why are complex systems so important? Because the reason AI is so powerful is that its intelligence emerges during training. These things all come from the concept of complex systems. The entire financial market is also essentially an extremely large complex system. Its evolution has some degree of computational irreducibility, which makes it very difficult to predict. But that unpredictability is also what makes it fascinating.

## Full Analysis of AI Supply-Chain Bottlenecks: HBM, Optical Modules, and Power - Which Is Most Scarce?

**Mr. Z:** Recently you have been talking a lot about GPUs, HBM, optical modules, power, data centers, and other topics. Among these different complex systems, what kinds of relationships do you see? What do you think is the biggest challenge or bottleneck right now?

**Iamai:** The long-term bottleneck is actually a shortage of electricity. In the short term, there are many bottlenecks, meaning all kinds of frictions in the supply chain. The biggest bottleneck right now should be HBM, high bandwidth memory, and then optical modules. The real explosion in optical modules may come next year, a bit later. There are still quite a few shortages across the whole supply chain, but in the long run, electricity will be scarce.

**Mr. Z:** You just said optical modules have not truly exploded yet. Can you talk about the current size of the optical-module market and how you see its future growth? My understanding is that optical modules were a market that was only really realized around 2024, but recently there seem to be many trends being hyped, such as AAOI or LITE.

**Iamai:** Optical modules are essentially like the network cable we use at home, except their bandwidth is higher. At home, you may use gigabit bandwidth, which is 1G. Now an optical module is usually 800G or 1.6T, equivalent to 800 times or 1,600 times your home gigabit bandwidth.

Optical modules mainly increase the bandwidth interconnection between different machines. Why did it explode before? Because the scale of compute nodes in data centers had not yet reached that level before. It was still in the relatively early stage of exponential growth, and the demand for interconnection had not fully manifested. As GPU shipments rise, the need for interconnection between compute nodes will continue to increase, and then we will see optical-module demand explode.

Actually, GPUs, memory, and optical modules all grow together, but their growth coefficients have some subtle differences. This leads to the time lag we see in the market among the growth trends of these three different components. The explosion in memory actually began only around last October. Optical modules may be slightly later than memory.

**Victor:** When optical modules are in short supply, which parts of the supply chain will that transmit to next? For example, PCBs, heat dissipation, liquid cooling, and so on.

**Iamai:** If you look at the post I published today, it is the BOM table for the architectural evolution from GB300 to Rubin. You can actually see some clues. At the moment, the demand explosion in memory still looks the clearest. Optical modules are more related to NVLink and network chips, but their growth rate is only about 120%, while the growth rate in memory demand is 400%. So from an investment perspective, investing in memory may make it easier to get a big result. However, optical modules may be reflected more at the overall data-center level, between racks and racks. That BOM table may not fully reflect it.

**Victor:** So the big meat is still in HBM?

**Iamai:** Yes.

**Mr. Z:** What causes the growth coefficients of optical modules and memory to differ? What is the key factor?

**Iamai:** From a relatively macro perspective, it is actually user demand. Especially the recent explosion of agentic workloads, which has caused memory demand to explode. Users' contexts are getting larger and larger, and the amount of content that needs to be processed is increasing. So the amount of KV cache required will become larger and larger.

The explosion of optical modules is because the demand for long-distance cross-rack communication between racks in data centers continues to increase. The density of GPU compute we can stack inside a single rack has an upper limit right now, because heat dissipation cannot be stacked infinitely. We can only spread it out by expanding the number of racks. So optical modules are essentially a key component for connecting distributed systems.

As AI applications develop, training has reached a relatively slower-growth region of the scaling law. The explosion of inference demand will then cause demand for memory and optical modules to explode along with it. CPU demand later is actually also starting to slowly emerge.

## The Return of CPU Demand and the Debate Over Custom Chips as "Picking Up Sesame Seeds While Dropping the Watermelon"

**Mr. Z:** Lisa Su is also saying that AI data centers are what everyone will need to build up in the future, so it will no longer just be the world of GPUs; CPUs are coming back. What do you think of AMD's positioning in CPUs?

**Iamai:** The core is still that some agentic work is starting to increase CPU demand. I previously discussed this with friends. From the perspective of running Claude Code locally, when doing multiple tasks, CPU does not seem to be a particularly big bottleneck. But at the data-center level, because of the diversity and complexity of agentic work, users may need a lot of non-neural-network computation. There will be more simulation-type computation. Those logic computations need to process the logical relationship between AI and the real world.

**Victor:** On the IC-design track, you previously posted that ASIC custom chips are "self-entertainment." How do you see that? For example, in Taiwan equities, MediaTek received Google's TPU order and its stock price rose very sharply. How do you view the custom-chip track?

**Iamai:** Custom chips definitely have their value. It is just that many people think custom chips mean we do not need GPUs anymore, and that view may not be very correct. Especially for startups, for example, Midjourney's founder spent a year adapting to TPUs, but in the end may look back and realize that the input-output ratio would have been higher if that time had been spent on NVIDIA.

Unless you know very clearly what your needs are, and can customize your ASIC in a very targeted way to reduce costs. But the question is whether this cost is suitable for startups. On top of that, the whole development speed of AI is now extremely fast. If you spend your energy and time on those things, are you not picking up sesame seeds while dropping the watermelon?

## Frontier-Model Competition: Recursive Self-Improvement Is the Right Path

**Victor:** Yesterday there was news that OpenAI's Karpathy moved to Anthropic. How do you think competition among these models will evolve, and how will it affect the hardware supply chain?

**Iamai:** The strongest right now should be Claude Code, Opus, and Claude. Their abilities in work scenarios and coding are still the strongest. GPT-5.5 is actually not bad in this regard either. The chase among frontier labs is still quite tight.

But I feel that, especially Google, the new model they released a few days ago seems to have received poor reviews. Although its speed is several times faster than GPT-5.5, the actual perceived experience is not very good. I think Google's direction may be a bit problematic. They want to build a multimodal model that can connect the user's input and output any-to-any. xAI's direction also seems a bit off.

I think the most core and most critical thing for frontier models is long-horizon task consistency. Everything should revolve around this, rather than pursuing things like multimodality. Once your long-horizon task consistency can be achieved, AI can realize recursive self-improvement, and your R&D costs will drop significantly. You do not need so many AI researchers. You only need to set a sufficiently clear goal, and a recursive self-improvement system can automatically achieve that goal.

This is also why Claude is so strong. They see this direction very accurately. Many other frontier AI labs have been misled by the market and have not gone in the right direction, so they cannot beat Claude. Including why Karpathy went to Anthropic: because he previously built something called AutoResearch, a recursive self-improvement system. I think this releases a very significant signal: the path Claude is taking is the right one.

## Token Economics: The First Principles of AI Investment

**Mr. Z:** So for these frontier large models, in the end it all comes down to tokens. Recently we often talk about Token Economics. What is this most related to? GPU utilization, KV cache, or token latency? Which metric is the most important?

**Iamai:** Token essentially means: how much money do I put in, and how much money can I get back? The simplest model is: if I invest one dollar in AI, can I earn back two dollars? Only when one dollar invested returns two dollars can you realize a recursive self-improvement system. Otherwise it is purely losing money. What you pursue in economics is to put in one cent and ultimately produce two cents of return. Only then can the whole AI game continue. Otherwise, no matter how you toss and turn, it is wrong.

**Mr. Z:** So this is the most important metric for future investment targets? Do traditional chip benchmarks still matter?

**Iamai:** I think that thing is not important. The most core thing is still your ability to process a long-term task. That task may not necessarily be very difficult, but it requires long-term persistence and the ability to maintain a certain consistency while running.

A simple example is investing. Investing is actually something that places great emphasis on long-term goal consistency. Why were Buffett and Munger's long-term returns so high? Because they kept running the concept of value investing. Being able to run something for a long time is actually extremely difficult. When you operate as a rational person in the market, you will see all kinds of noise and all kinds of voices interfering with you. But if you grasp the inner core and do things according to it, that kind of consistency requires a certain talent.

## Where Is the U.S. AI Bull Market Now? Top Signals and Portfolio Strategy

**Mr. Z:** Do you think the current overall market environment is bull or bear? What stage has the U.S. stock AI trade reached?

**Iamai:** From a trading perspective, there are definitely localized overheated sectors right now. Memory was definitely overheated a while ago, and hasn't it been pulling back these two days? Optical modules are the same. They rose tenfold in a few months, so that is definitely overheated. A phase adjustment is inevitable. But thinking from the perspective of the future, we are still at a very early stage, because future demand for computation actually has no upper limit.

**Mr. Z:** From 2023 until now, where exactly are we in this AI bull market? Is the bubble about to burst? Or are we at a top signal?

**Iamai:** It is hard for me to see a top signal. I have a very clear top signal: when the price of energy rises to an unaffordable degree, that must be a stage top. For example, if electricity prices jump, supply completely cannot meet demand, even civilian electricity becomes completely insufficient, all electricity has to go to satisfying AI data-center demand, and data centers also frequently lose power and do not have enough supply. That kind of situation must be a bubble, and all investment should stop.

**Mr. Z:** From a portfolio-management perspective, how would you allocate the weighting among different sectors? Optical modules, power, GPU, CPU vendors - how would you rank them?

**Iamai:** My relatively large position at the moment may still be in NVIDIA, because the certainty of its future growth is the highest. If you want to seek high returns within a smaller range, you can look at memory, optical modules, CPUs, and so on. Actually, it is also simple now: as long as those targets fall to around the 5-day or 14-day moving average, if you do not have a position, adding some near the moving average is actually a relatively good choice. Because these things have an internal growth rate and usually grow along the weekly or monthly line. When is it overheated? When your price deviates too far from its internal growth curve and the curvature is too high, that is a bubble. At that time, you may need to consider reducing positions or doing some defensive operations.

**Mr. Z:** What would your defensive operation be? Buying gold or Treasury bonds?

**Iamai:** Either is fine. Personally, I do not really like gold because it has no productivity. Buying gold is essentially not very different from Bitcoin, except that central banks recognize it more. But its value is still mostly built on consensus among people. For risk-off, I may generally just hold cash, then add positions again when the adjustment near the moving average is completed.

## Bitcoin in the Post-AGI Era: A Stabilizer and Hedge for Wealth

**Mr. Z:** You mentioned Bitcoin, which fits very well with the "three-horse carriage" investment theory that 168X recently proposed. In the AI era, compute is first. Whether you invest in stocks or Bitcoin, you are actually tying yourself to compute. How do you see Bitcoin's future?

**Iamai:** In a post-AGI era, the value of Bitcoin may become larger and larger. Why do some people see Bitcoin rising to $10 million per coin? If Bitcoin can rise to that price, it must mean that the wealth of the entire world at that time has reached an extremely massive level.

Why can it reach that level? I think it is very likely to be driven by the development of AI. Because human intelligence and ability actually have certain upper limits. You see that today GPT-5.5 solved a mathematical problem that had existed for 80 years, combining mathematics from two completely different fields and solving that problem. That paper can directly be published in a top mathematics journal.

Carbon-based organisms have their own limitations, which means that we have already reached a bottleneck in pushing the economy upward. The next stage may be driven by AI. When AI drives the wealth of the whole society to a certain scale, you must need something to carry that wealth. Otherwise, that wealth will either be diluted by inflation, or it can only be invested back into industry, but that would lead to excessive volatility and trigger economic turbulence.

Bitcoin may serve such a role: a stabilizer. To some extent, Bitcoin may even hedge certain extreme risks, such as the risk of AI losing control. In the future, AI will become stronger and stronger, and it is highly probable that it will control the economic lifeline of the entire world. Then how can humans argue with AI? How can we obtain our own sovereignty? Bitcoin may be a relatively good choice.

**Victor:** So Bitcoin is not only a hedge against the U.S. dollar fiat system; it can also be a kind of hedge in the AI era? How do you view this kind of hedging combination?

**Iamai:** This kind of hedge is necessary to some extent. AI is indeed developing very fast. If you are all-in AI, you need to bear extremely large volatility. That volatility may not be very good for mental health. If you diversify your assets a bit, it may be easier for you to do this for the long term.

**Victor:** A few days ago you posted that some high-frequency quant firms are doing high-frequency extraction in the crypto market, transferring liquidity into semiconductors, and you suggested that everyone withdraw their BTC from exchanges and put it in their own wallets. How did you observe this phenomenon?

**Iamai:** Because I previously developed crypto quant strategies with friends. We observed that, including before last year, overall crypto liquidity was still very good. But after a collapse last year, it was very obvious that overall crypto liquidity became extremely poor. There may be institutions and strategies better than ours continuously extracting liquidity from it. So if you are a BTC holder, do not provide liquidity to the whole market. Keep it safe.

## Cloud Compute: The Real Barriers to AI Data Centers, Seen Through Mining Companies' Transformation

**Victor:** How do you view the cloud-compute track? For example, companies like Oracle that are actively investing CapEx to build AI data centers.

**Iamai:** In cloud compute, I used to be relatively bullish on IREN, because their execution capability in the physical world is very strong. Maybe because they came from Bitcoin mining farms, they locked in a large amount of power orders in advance. But later I gradually realized that the value of companies such as CoreWeave (CRWV) and Nebius (NBIS) may have been underestimated by me.

Why did my thinking change? Because of xAI. It was able to deploy 100,000 GPUs within 20 days. Why can't other teams do that? Also, why can xAI's compute be sold relatively well to Anthropic, while IREN seems to find it hard to do the same?

I think IREN's role may lean toward being good at civil construction work. But AI inference requires extremely complex cooling systems, which may be quite different from Bitcoin mining. Also, the internal networking and management of AI data centers are far more complex than Bitcoin mining.

Some time ago, I saw Jane Street, the quant firm that made around $40 billion to $50 billion last year, show their data center. You could see they used NVIDIA Blackwell. The cooling system paired with each Blackwell rack was extremely large. It looks like just one rack, but the supporting cooling, power supply, network switches, and those things are actually an extremely complicated whole system. Building the shell of a data center is easy, but getting the machines inside set up and running stably is harder.

**Victor:** What do you think is the main difference between a company like IREN, which is transforming from Bitcoin mining into AI cloud computing, and companies that originally did data centers?

**Iamai:** I think the difference mainly lies in their understanding of AI. Teams like CoreWeave may have more AI-related R&D personnel. IREN also acquired an AI inference company some time ago to strengthen this capability. I think if those mining companies want to transform later, they will also need to strengthen this capability.

## Can Chinese Memory Replicate the Solar Miracle? Where Is YMTC's Ceiling?

**Victor:** On the China side, memory companies such as YMTC are also preparing to go public recently. You previously posted that China is preparing to "kill memory the way it killed solar, driving token prices down to cabbage prices." Do you think this is very likely to happen? How competitive is Chinese memory?

**Iamai:** China's production capacity is extremely strong. Things that may limit memory output in other countries do not exist on China's side. China does not lack electricity, nor does it lack construction capability. The entire upstream and downstream support system can coordinate perfectly. The only possible issue is that, in the later stages, the number of lithography machines may not necessarily keep up. That may be its ceiling.

When I say it can kill memory like solar, that may only be a kind of vision. The reason solar could keep adding capacity is that solar cells have essentially become very commoditized, and solar conversion efficiency has an upper limit. But although memory is also a commodity, it has different layers. The highest-end memory is HBM, and HBM is divided into HBM2, HBM3, HBM3E, HBM4, and can continue stacking upward. On the GPU side, demand for high-bandwidth memory is: the faster and the more, the better. It is not like solar, where once a piece of land is covered with solar panels, you do not need to touch it for many years. The upper limit for stacking memory upward will be extremely high. Solar capacity will definitely eventually become excessive, but memory may not necessarily.

Also, solar has the problem of day and night light exposure. It generates power during the day and not at night. This kind of fluctuation has a relatively large impact on the grid and requires a large amount of energy storage. Unless you put solar into space, but space is another topic.

## Space Data Centers and Semiconductor Pullbacks: Where Is the Next Breakout Point?

**Victor:** Speaking of space, how do you view the space-data-center track? SpaceX is recently preparing to go public. Will the space sector explode? Are there any subsectors you recommend paying attention to?

**Iamai:** Space will definitely explode at some point in the future. If we keep building data centers on the surface of the Earth, we will eventually run into many problems: near-term approval and regulatory processes, and constraints on power supply. So data centers will inevitably have to move into space.

As Starship succeeds and the cost of space launch comes down, the feasibility of space AI data centers will become higher and higher. Space data centers have one big problem: heat dissipation. I think when it is about to explode, positioning in some space-cooling stocks may be a relatively good choice. Recently I have been paying attention to a company called CPSH, a very small company, still under research. Because space cooling does not yet have a relatively certain solution, but this problem will definitely appear in the future. Space data centers will also require a large amount of communication demand. This communication is actually laser communication demand, which can to some extent connect with today's optical-communication sector.

**Victor:** Recently the community has also been discussing whether a semiconductor pullback is bullish for software stocks whose valuations were heavily cut at the beginning of the year. Do you think software companies have an opportunity to make a comeback in the near term, or will the future main line still be in the hardware supply chain?

**Iamai:** I think the whole constraint is still concentrated at the physical-world level. The constraints at the software level are relatively not that strong. Of course, there are also good companies in software, but from an opportunity-cost perspective, it may still be better and more visible to first solve the physical-world constraints, then look at software.

## Survival Rules for the AI Era: Live Long Enough, and You Win

**Victor:** In the AI era, how can ordinary people or white-collar workers face this era? Should they become more familiar with using AI tools, or participate in the hardware supply chain? How can they put themselves in a relatively favorable position?

**Iamai:** You can learn to use AI more. The ability to collaborate with AI will definitely be extremely important. It can greatly increase your productivity and help you complete more things and solve more problems. On the other hand, to some extent you also need to hold some relevant assets, ensuring that you can share some benefits from future trends. You can do both of these things, and they do not conflict.

**Victor:** On your homepage, you built a data dashboard. Can you share how you built this product? How did you use AI to build your own product and continuous cash flow?

**Iamai:** This is a small product of mine. Because I track the entire AI industry quite a lot, I discovered that the AI industry is actually even more complex than the semiconductor supply chain. Optical modules may have only started rising in the past two years. There are also power semiconductors, cooling, and many other subfields. Tracking such a huge industry chain is very complex and troublesome.

So I wanted to build a product that could visually and intuitively show where the bottlenecks in the whole industry chain are. After I know where the bottlenecks are, it may be more helpful for me to hold my assets for the long term, to know what stage they are at now and what the future trend looks like. That makes it easier to hold for the long term and avoid some short-term emotional trades. It is still based on data-driven decision-making.

Basically, it was built with Codex. The most core thing was solving the data-source problem. Brokerages such as Longbridge provide APIs. You can directly give the documentation to Codex, let it connect to the API, and then have it customize dashboard functions according to your needs.

**Mr. Z:** I am quite curious. After AI causes productivity to explode, will there be a situation where, in the future, there is universal basic income, like Musk says, and money is no longer important? Under that situation, how should we remeasure the relationship between productivity and capital?

**Iamai:** To some extent, the later development of AI may in some sense eliminate capitalism. Once AI realizes ASI, artificial superintelligence, or AGI, it can enter the economic system of all human society and regulate the global economy from a very macro perspective. You actually do not really need those so-called investors to go find alpha. AI can see through, at a glance, where the current bottlenecks in the entire society are, and then it will directly go do it.

**Mr. Z:** So the real future wealth is obtaining priority rights to mobilize resources such as AI compute, energy, and robots?

**Iamai:** At a certain stage, this is very important, but after that stage it may not be important either. Before AI achieves recursive self-improvement, these things are still useful. Once AI can fully recursively self-improve, sometimes I myself feel a kind of confusion: once we pass a certain node, the resources and permissions you supposedly control may also become unimportant.

The most core thing is still what you need. I did a calculation before: if in the future we really achieve a Kardashev-level civilization, almost everyone in the world could own luxury cars, yachts, and mansions. At that time, what is the meaning of the capital you control?

**Victor:** Finally, do you have anything you would like to share with the audience, whether about investing, life, or survival?

**Iamai:** I think the best thing is to live as long as possible. Because as long as you can live long enough, you are more likely to see the arrival of that Singularity. At that time, you may not need anything. You are just there, and then you can have everything.

**Mr. Z:** So basically in the AI era, no matter how hard you try, you cannot beat compute or AI, but the only thing you can do is go to bed early, get up early, eat well, and sleep well?

**Iamai:** Yes. Be happy every day. No matter what, in the end you will not lose. Anyway, take good care of your body.
